The salt that made Zigong rich and the dinosaurs that made it famous come from the same geological gift. The red sandstone of the Sichuan Basin sealed away both the brine left by ancient evaporating seas and the bones of Mesozoic reptiles. The salt lies deep; the bones lie nearer the surface — and at **Dashanpu**, northeast of the city, you can dig almost anywhere and strike remains 160 million years old.

In the 1980s geologists uncovered an unusually complete **Middle-Jurassic** graveyard here: not scattered fragments of a single dinosaur, but an entire fauna — fish, turtles, crocodile relatives, and flying reptiles included — densely preserved in the **Shaximiao Formation**. This "whole-community" preservation is rare almost everywhere else, which is why the site's diversity is considered, by some measures, unmatched in the world.

Rather than ship the fossils elsewhere, China built the museum directly over the bonebed. The **Zigong Dinosaur Museum opened in 1987** — the first dinosaur museum in Asia and one of only a handful in the world set on the dig itself. Many remarkable species have been found here: the absurdly long-necked **Omeisaurus**, the **Gigantspinosaurus** with its enormous shoulder spines, and the region's big Jurassic predator, **Yangchuanosaurus**.

So a city built on salt acquired a second identity as the "home of dinosaurs." It is not an arbitrary pairing: it is exactly why the festival here is the *Dinosaur* Lantern Festival, and why luminous sauropods drift above the New Year crowds. Salt and dinosaurs are the two deep, improbable inheritances the same ground handed this city.
