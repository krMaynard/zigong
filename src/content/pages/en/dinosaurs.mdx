The salt that made Zigong rich and the dinosaurs that made it famous come from the same geological gift. The red sandstone of the Sichuan Basin sealed away both the brine left by ancient evaporating seas and the bones of Mesozoic reptiles. The salt lies deep; the bones lie nearer the surface — and at **Dashanpu**, northeast of the city, you can dig almost anywhere and strike remains 160 million years old.

In the 1980s geologists uncovered an unusually complete **Middle-Jurassic** graveyard here: not scattered fragments of a single dinosaur, but an entire fauna — fish, turtles, crocodile relatives, and flying reptiles included — densely preserved in the **Shaximiao Formation**. This "whole-community" preservation is rare almost everywhere else, which is why the site's diversity is considered, by some measures, unmatched in the world. Rather than ship the fossils elsewhere, China built the museum directly over the bonebed: the **Zigong Dinosaur Museum opened in 1987**, the first dinosaur museum in Asia and one of only a handful in the world set on the dig itself.

## The dinosaurs named for the city

Among the many dinosaurs found here, two carry the name of this very ground — a local stamp that fossils elsewhere rarely bear.

**Zigongosaurus** ("Zigong lizard"), named in 1976, is a medium-sized sauropod some fifteen metres long. It is almost a microcosm of the puzzle of Jurassic Chinese sauropods: the material is fragmentary and its identity is still argued over — some fold it into *Omeisaurus*, some into *Mamenchisaurus*, and some insist it stands as its own genus. That a single name can sustain an argument this long says something true about the bonebed: it has left palaeontology nearly as many questions as answers.

**Dashanpusaurus**, named for the township where it was dug up, is known from a skeleton lacking its skull. Its significance runs deeper than the local name: recent re-examination argues it may be among the **earliest-diverging macronarians** — and earliest known neosauropods — yet found, implying that this great branch of later dinosaurs originated earlier, and ranged more widely, than once thought. A fossil beneath a small Sichuan town, in other words, may have rewritten the timetable for a whole lineage of dinosaurs.

The museum holds other celebrated species too, though their names come from elsewhere: the absurdly long-necked **Omeisaurus** (named for Mount Emei), the **Gigantspinosaurus** with its enormous shoulder spines (named for the feature), and the region's big Jurassic predator, **Yangchuanosaurus** (named for Yongchuan).

So a city built on salt acquired a second identity as the "home of dinosaurs." It is not an arbitrary pairing: it is exactly why the festival here is the *Dinosaur* Lantern Festival, and why luminous sauropods drift above the New Year crowds. Salt and dinosaurs are the two deep, improbable inheritances the same ground handed this city.
