Zigong's story begins with something invisible. The salt here is not in the sea or on the surface but dissolved in brine hundreds, even a thousand, metres underground — often alongside natural gas. To reach it you must first drill a hole toward the centre of the earth. And so a landlocked city turned well-boring into a craft, handed down across generations.

## Drilling toward the centre of the earth

From the Song dynasty onward, local salt workers mastered **percussion ("churn") drilling**: a heavy iron bit on a cable twisted from bamboo, raised and dropped from tall wooden derricks (the *tianche*, "sky carriages") until it had pounded its way through rock, the shaft then lined with sections of jointed bamboo. Wells were not bored in a single pass — bits snapped and cables parted, so the drillers developed an entire family of "fishing" tools to retrieve whatever had fallen to the bottom. This toolkit — boring, casing, and downhole recovery — predated comparable Western methods by centuries, and is sometimes called the "fifth great invention" of ancient China and a forerunner of modern oil drilling.

At its height the skyline bristled with a thousand derricks, the tallest over a hundred metres, lashed together from fir without a single nail. Brine raised from the wells ran through bamboo flumes strung overhead to the boiling houses; the natural gas that came up with it was piped off to fire the pans that boiled the brine down to salt. An integrated energy-and-chemicals operation, run on bamboo, wood, and muscle, was working day and night here centuries ago.

**In 1835 the Shenhai well reached about 1,001 metres** — the first borehole in human history to pass a kilometre, drilled with nothing but bamboo, wood, and muscle. It produced both ingredients of salt-making at once: the **brine** to boil and the **natural gas** to boil it with. Shenhai still makes salt by traditional means today.

</div>

## The city that salt built

Salt shaped not only the landscape but the society. Drawing brine, conveying it, boiling it, and shipping the salt formed a chain that supported drillers, furnace-masters, boatmen, banks, and trading houses. Merchants from Shaanxi and Shanxi amassed fortunes here and fixed that wealth in guildhalls and mansions — the finest of them, the **Xiqin Guildhall**, is today the home of the Salt Industry History Museum. The name "Zigong" is itself a salt story: it fuses a character from each of the district's two most famous salt sources, **Ziliujing** and **Gongjing**.

## A salt merchant's estate: the Wang family courtyard

The wealth salt produced did not only raise guildhalls; it raised family estates. The largest to survive is the **Wang Family Estate** (also the Zicheng Ancestral Hall) at Cibao'ao, in today's Yantan District.

Its builder, **Wang Hefu** (1867–1930), was a *rushang* — a scholar-merchant — of the **Wang Sanwei lineage**, foremost of the "four great salt houses" of Qing-dynasty Zigong (the Wang, Li, Hu, and Yan houses). In 1890 he and his brothers began the estate to honour their father, Wang Zicheng; it took six years and was finished in 1896. The compound covers some eleven *mu* (about 4,500 m² of building) — a Sichuan column-and-tie timber mansion of four linked courtyards, approached by a forty-metre stone path to a five-metre half-moon pool, with a grove of nanmu beside it; more than 600 carvings in stone, wood, and stucco survive.

The most telling touch is a stone plaque above the gate: **"a hut among men"** (*jié lú rén jìng*), from Tao Yuanming's line, "I built my hut among men, yet hear no clatter of carriage and horse." A man rich on salt chose a recluse-poet's words for his own lintel. Beyond the house, Wang petitioned the Fushun county office to establish a township around it and laid out a market street, with market days on every 1st, 4th, and 7th of the lunar ten-day cycle — a single estate that grew a small town.

The courtyards then absorbed layer upon layer of modern history: raided by bandits in 1911 but saved through Wang's efforts; a base for the Communist underground in the 1920s–30s; and, from 1923, home to the "Wang Estate School." In 2020 it was listed among Zigong's first protected well-salt heritage sites and is being restored — slated to become a museum of Salt-Gang cuisine.

## The salt capital at war

In the twentieth century, salt gave Zigong a chapter both heavy and proud. After war came and the coastal salt fields fell, Sichuan well-salt became a lifeline for unoccupied China; production was driven hard. It was under that wartime pressure that Ziliujing and Gongjing were merged into a single municipality in 1939 — the formal birth of Zigong.

As a strategic industry supplying the front, the salt capital became a bombing target. According to the inscription on the city's memorial, between October 1939 and August 1941 Japanese aircraft struck in seven raids — more than 480 sorties dropping over 1,500 bombs — destroying more than 2,700 buildings, killing or wounding over a thousand people, and damaging the salt works themselves. Even so, the city's giving led the nation: answering General Feng Yuxiang's patriotic donation drive, Zigong alone contributed more than 120 million yuan, the highest of any place in China. (That "120 million" was wartime *fabi*, amid runaway inflation, so any present-day figure is only an order of magnitude: by purchasing power, perhaps a couple of million US dollars today — though at the time it was enough to buy the front two warplanes.)

After the war, salt slowly gave ground to other industries in the national economy, but it never truly left. Shenhai still yields salt, the Xiqin Guildhall has become the museum that tells the story, and "salt capital" remains the phrase by which the city understands itself.

> **In safety, remember danger; never forget national humiliation.**
>
> The great war of resistance was a cause the whole nation took up. In the salt capital of Ziliujing the people threw themselves forward, hearts set on saving the country, labouring day and night, the salt industry's great wealth sustaining the front… And so came the enemy's ravages: between October 1939 and August 1941, Japanese aircraft — more than 480 sorties in seven waves of indiscriminate bombing — dropped over 1,500 bombs, destroyed more than 2,700 buildings, killed or wounded over a thousand people, and damaged the salt wells. The dead lay across the scorched earth; the living were scattered… Soldiers and civilians shared one hatred of the foe. Answering General Feng Yuxiang's patriotic donation campaign, contributions here exceeded 120 million — first in the nation… This stele is raised that our countrymen and the world may remember.
>
> — September 1997
