Zigong's story begins with something invisible. The salt here is not in the sea or on the surface but dissolved in brine hundreds, even a thousand, metres underground — often alongside natural gas. To reach it you must first drill a hole toward the centre of the earth. And so a landlocked city turned well-boring into a craft, handed down across generations.

From the Song dynasty onward, local salt workers mastered **percussion ("churn") drilling**: a heavy iron bit on a cable twisted from bamboo, raised and dropped again and again until it had pounded its way through rock, the shaft lined with lengths of bamboo casing. This toolkit — boring, casing, and fishing broken tools from the bottom — predated comparable Western methods by centuries, and is sometimes called the "fifth great invention" of ancient China and a forerunner of modern oil drilling.

<div class="factbox">

**In 1835 the Shenhai well reached about 1,001 metres** — the first borehole in human history to pass a kilometre, drilled with nothing but bamboo, wood, and muscle. It produced both ingredients of salt-making at once: the **brine** to boil and the **natural gas** to boil it with. Shenhai still makes salt by traditional means today.

</div>

Salt also shaped the look of the city. At its height the skyline bristled with a thousand wooden derricks; the merchants who worked them amassed startling fortunes and fixed that wealth in guildhalls and mansions. The name "Zigong" is itself a fusion of its two most famous salt sources — **Ziliujing** and **Gongjing**.

In the twentieth century, salt gave Zigong a heavier chapter. After war came and the coastal salt fields fell, Sichuan well-salt became a lifeline for unoccupied China; production was driven hard. It was under that wartime pressure that Ziliujing and Gongjing were merged into a single municipality in 1939 — the formal birth of Zigong. As a strategic war industry it was also bombed by Japanese aircraft, an episode a memorial wall in the city records under the words "never forget national humiliation."

After the war, salt slowly gave ground to other industries in the national economy, but it never truly left. Shenhai still yields salt, the Xiqin Guildhall has become the museum that tells the story, and "salt capital" remains the phrase by which the city understands itself.
